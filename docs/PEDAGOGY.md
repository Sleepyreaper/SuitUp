# SuitUp Teaching Rationale

Version: 0.1.0
Last updated: July 4, 2026
Audience: Product builders, curriculum writers, and beginners who want to understand how SuitUp teaches

## Purpose

This document explains how SuitUp teaches a total beginner to play American Mah Jongg and why the first release uses original practice patterns instead of reproducing copyrighted National Mah Jongg League (NMJL) card text.

SuitUp is not just a rules reference. It is a patient, offline teacher that guides a learner from unopened-tile confusion to one complete playable hand.

## Teaching Goal

The teaching goal is simple:

Take a learner from:
"I have a physical set of tiles and no idea what any of this means."

To:
"I can set up the table, identify the tiles, choose East, build the walls, receive a hand, complete the Charleston correctly, understand group vocabulary, and play through a full practice hand against computer opponents."

This is a mechanics-first curriculum.

## What the App Must Teach First

A total beginner does not need abstract strategy on minute one. A total beginner needs orientation.

SuitUp therefore starts with concrete, physical concepts before asking the learner to make decisions.

The first-release curriculum progresses in this order:

1. Tile-set orientation
2. Physical table setup
3. Dealer selection
4. Wall building
5. Dealing
6. Charleston execution
7. Group recognition
8. Joker limits
9. Draw and discard flow
10. One full playable practice hand
11. Repeated play against AI at increasing difficulty

This order is intentional. It mirrors the learner's actual confusion curve.

## Why the Curriculum Starts with the Physical Set

A beginner looking at an American Mah Jongg set often does not know:
- how many tiles there should be,
- which tiles belong to which family,
- which tiles are suits versus honors,
- or what special tiles like flowers and jokers are for.

So SuitUp begins with the 152-tile set itself:
- 108 suit tiles,
- 16 winds,
- 12 dragons,
- 8 flowers,
- 8 jokers.

This gives the learner a reliable inventory before anything else happens.

Pedagogically, this matters because anxiety drops when the learner can sort what they see into named categories.

## Why Dealer Selection Is Taught Early

Dealer selection by East draw appears early because it gives the learner an immediate, memorable ritual with a clear outcome.

The learner sees that:
- seats matter,
- East matters,
- and the dealer has a special starting role.

Teaching East early also prepares the learner for the next mechanical facts:
- East receives 14 tiles,
- other players receive 13,
- and turn flow depends on this starting asymmetry.

In other words, East is not trivia. East is structural.

## Why Wall Building Comes Before Hand Reading

SuitUp teaches the four walls before it teaches pattern reading because beginners need to know where the tiles physically come from.

The curriculum therefore makes the learner practice building:
- four walls,
- each 19 tiles long,
- each 2 tiles high.

This step turns a pile of tiles into an understandable game surface.

For a beginner, that is a major comprehension jump. The game stops looking random and starts looking organized.

## Why the Charleston Is Taught as a Fixed Procedure

Many beginners find the Charleston intimidating because it feels social, procedural, and easy to mess up.

SuitUp removes that ambiguity by teaching it as a strict sequence:
- first pass right,
- second pass across,
- third pass left,
- then courtesy pass left,
- courtesy pass across,
- courtesy pass right.

The curriculum treats the Charleston as a procedure to learn by repetition, not as an improvisational exchange.

This is important for two reasons.

First, procedural confidence reduces fear. A learner who knows the order can participate without freezing.

Second, a fixed sequence is teachable in software. The app can guide the learner one pass at a time and explain that prohibitions apply, so legal movement matters.

## Why Group Vocabulary Comes Before Broad Strategy

SuitUp teaches pair, pung, kong, quint, and sextet as core vocabulary before deeper strategic judgment.

That is deliberate.

Beginners cannot evaluate a hand if they do not recognize what kinds of groups they are trying to assemble. Group names give shape to the hand-building process.

This also supports the AI practice experience. When the app explains a move, it can describe what the learner is assembling in plain terms:
- a pair,
- a pung,
- a kong,
- a quint,
- or a sextet.

Vocabulary creates mental handles. Without those handles, every hand feels like chaos.

## Why Joker Limits Are Taught Explicitly

New players often assume jokers are unrestricted wild tiles.

SuitUp corrects that assumption early.

The curriculum teaches that joker limits exist and that legal use matters. This prevents a common beginner error: learning a simplified game that later has to be unlearned.

That teaching choice reflects a broader principle:
it is better to teach a bounded mechanic correctly than to teach a looser version that feels easier but creates future confusion.

## Why SuitUp Uses Original Practice Patterns

SuitUp does not reproduce the copyrighted text of the National Mah Jongg League card.

That boundary is both legal and instructional.

### Legal reason

The NMJL card is copyrighted material. SuitUp should not copy, restate, display, or approximate protected card text as if it were app content.

### Teaching reason

A total beginner does not need the full burden of copyrighted card text on the first lesson.

What the learner needs first is:
- tile recognition,
- setup fluency,
- passing fluency,
- group recognition,
- legal joker awareness,
- and confidence completing a hand-shaped objective.

Original practice patterns let the app teach those mechanics directly.

That creates a cleaner on-ramp:
- the learner understands how a hand is assembled,
- the learner experiences turn flow,
- the learner practices reading groups,
- and the learner can complete playable exercises offline.

Later, the learner can bring that mechanical confidence to a legally obtained NMJL card.

## Why Original Practice Patterns Are Better for Beginners

Original practice patterns are not a compromise. They are a teaching tool.

They help because they:
- isolate one mechanic at a time,
- avoid overwhelming the learner with dense card formatting,
- let the app stage difficulty gradually,
- and allow examples to be written in simpler beginner language.

For example, a beginner lesson can focus on recognizing a pair and a pung without also forcing the learner to parse a dense external reference card.

This is how good instruction works: remove unnecessary complexity from the first encounter, then add realism after the foundation is solid.

## Beginner-First Progression

SuitUp's beginner-first progression is intentionally linear at the start and intentionally replayable later.

### Stage 1: "What is in the box?"
The learner identifies the tile families and confirms the 152-tile inventory.

### Stage 2: "How do we sit down?"
The learner understands four seats and the East draw for dealer selection.

### Stage 3: "How do we build the table?"
The learner builds four walls of 19 stacks by 2 tiles high.

### Stage 4: "How many tiles do we start with?"
The learner internalizes that East starts with 14 and the others start with 13.

### Stage 5: "What is the Charleston?"
The learner performs the exact passing order and understands that prohibitions exist.

### Stage 6: "What am I trying to make?"
The learner learns the meanings of pair, pung, kong, quint, and sextet.

### Stage 7: "How do jokers work?"
The learner learns that jokers are limited and governed, not free-form.

### Stage 8: "How do turns actually feel?"
The learner practices the draw-evaluate-discard loop.

### Stage 9: "Can I play a whole hand?"
The learner completes one full guided practice hand.

### Stage 10: "Can I improve?"
The learner plays repeated hands against AI opponents at three difficulty levels.

This progression supports confidence. Each stage answers one specific beginner question before moving to the next.

## Why AI Difficulty Should Scale Gradually

The project calls for three AI levels that can actually play.

From a teaching perspective, that matters because beginners benefit from controlled escalation.

A three-level AI structure supports:
- low-pressure first exposure,
- medium-pressure recognition practice,
- and stronger opposition once the learner understands flow.

This keeps the app useful after the first lesson. The learner does not outgrow the product the moment they understand setup.

## Offline Teaching Is a Feature, Not a Constraint

SuitUp runs locally and offline.

That is good pedagogy for this audience.

It means:
- no account creation,
- no network dependence,
- no waiting for remote services,
- no distraction from the learning loop.

The learner can put the physical set on the table, open the local app, and practice directly beside the tiles.

That tight physical-to-digital loop is exactly what a beginner teacher should support.

## What Success Looks Like

The first release succeeds if a beginner can do all of the following after working through the curriculum:

- identify the parts of a 152-tile American Mah Jongg set,
- determine dealer by East draw,
- build legal walls,
- deal correctly with East at 14 and others at 13,
- perform the Charleston in the taught order,
- recognize the major group types,
- understand that joker use is limited,
- and play through one full guided hand using original practice patterns.

If the learner can do those things, the app has done its job.

## Scope Boundary for This Curriculum

SuitUp teaches mechanics and confidence.

SuitUp does not claim to replace:
- a legally obtained NMJL card,
- advanced human table judgment,
- or every variation a player may encounter in social play.

That boundary should remain explicit in the user experience and in all future documentation.
