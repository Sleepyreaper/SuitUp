# SuitUp Beginner Pedagogy
Last updated: July 4, 2026
Applies to: SuitUp first release

This document defines how SuitUp teaches a total beginner to play American Mah Jongg with a physical set.

The teaching philosophy is simple:
one small step at a time, one clear action at the end of each lesson.

A beginner should never finish a lesson wondering, "What do I do with my hands now?"

Every lesson in SuitUp ends with a concrete physical or in-app action.

## Teaching philosophy

SuitUp is designed for someone who:
- owns a set
- may not know the tile names
- may not know how to build the walls
- may not know how a hand begins
- may feel overwhelmed by the NMJL card
- wants patient repetition without embarrassment

The curriculum therefore follows these principles:

### 1. Start with recognition before strategy

A beginner cannot make decisions with tiles they cannot name.

SuitUp teaches tile identity before hand planning.

### 2. Teach the table before teaching tactics

Before asking a learner to choose discards, teach:
- where tiles go
- how walls are built
- how dealer is chosen
- how many tiles each player gets
- what a turn looks like

### 3. Isolate one moving part at a time

American Mah Jongg includes many linked procedures:
the Charleston, calls, exposures, joker restrictions, and exact-card winning.

Beginners learn faster when each idea is introduced separately and then recombined.

### 4. End every lesson with a concrete action

Every lesson ends with something the learner does immediately:
- point to a tile
- sort a rack
- build a wall
- pass three tiles
- call an exposure
- decide whether a joker is legal
- complete one practice turn

This turns passive reading into active learning.

### 5. Teach confidence through constrained practice

The first goal is not expert play.
The first goal is calm, correct play.

SuitUp reduces decision load early, then gradually restores full game complexity.

### 6. Use plain language first

Terms like pung, kong, exposure, and redemption are taught, but only after the learner sees what those words mean.

Vocabulary follows experience, not the other way around.

### 7. Keep the NMJL card in view without copying it

SuitUp does not reproduce copyrighted card content.
Instead, it teaches the learner how to read and use their own current NMJL card.

The curriculum emphasizes:
- "pick a target hand"
- "check whether your tiles still fit it"
- "you only win with an exact card hand"

## Audience assumptions

This curriculum assumes the learner:
- is a complete beginner
- is using a physical American Mah Jongg set
- may be practicing alone before joining a real table
- benefits from repetition, visuals, and short lessons
- wants help that feels patient rather than judgey

## Instruction model

Each lesson uses the same structure:

1. Name the concept.
2. Show only the information needed for that concept.
3. Demonstrate the concept with a small example.
4. Ask the learner to do one concrete action.
5. Confirm success before unlocking the next lesson.

That consistency lowers anxiety.

## Curriculum sequence

The sequence below is mandatory for first-release beginner mode.

It moves from recognition, to setup, to pre-game procedures, to turn flow, to winning.

## Lesson 1: Identify tiles

### Goal

The learner can recognize the major tile categories without panic.

### Concepts taught

- Dots, Bams, Craks
- Winds
- Dragons
- Flowers
- Jokers
- The full set contains 152 tiles

### Why this comes first

A player who cannot tell a Bam from a Crak cannot follow any later instruction.

Tile identity is the alphabet of the game.

### Concrete action

Ask the learner to sort a mixed sample into:
- suits
- winds
- dragons
- flowers
- jokers

The lesson ends only when the learner physically or virtually places each tile into the correct category.

## Lesson 2: Name and compare tiles

### Goal

The learner can say what a tile is, not just vaguely recognize it.

### Concepts taught

- suit name
- rank number
- identical tiles versus merely similar tiles
- the difference between honors and suits

### Why it comes second

Before making groups, the learner must understand matching.

### Concrete action

Ask the learner to:
- name five shown tiles out loud or by clicking labels
- pick which tiles are exact matches
- separate identical tiles from non-identical lookalikes

## Lesson 3: Build the walls

### Goal

The learner can set up the table correctly.

### Concepts taught

- all tiles are mixed face down
- each player builds one wall
- each wall is 19 tiles long and 2 tiles high
- four walls form the square

### Why it comes here

Setup is tactile and confidence-building.
It gives the learner something real to do with the set before memorizing more rules.

### Concrete action

Ask the learner to build one correct wall:
19 stacks long and 2 tiles high.

The lesson ends when the learner completes one wall correctly and can explain that four such walls use all 152 tiles.

## Lesson 4: Choose the dealer

### Goal

The learner understands how the game starts with a dealer.

### Concepts taught

- dealer is chosen by wind draw
- East is dealer
- seats follow wind order

### Why it comes here

Dealer selection is the first table ritual that gives structure to the hand.

### Concrete action

Ask the learner to perform a mock wind draw and identify which player becomes dealer.

## Lesson 5: Deal the opening hands

### Goal

The learner knows the starting tile counts.

### Concepts taught

- dealer starts with 14 tiles
- all other players start with 13 tiles
- dealer discards first because dealer begins with one extra tile

### Why it comes here

The learner is now ready to see what a starting hand looks like.

### Concrete action

Ask the learner to count out:
- 14 tiles to dealer
- 13 tiles to one non-dealer

The lesson ends when the learner can state who starts with the extra tile and why.

## Lesson 6: Read the NMJL card as a target list

### Goal

The learner understands what the card is for without being overwhelmed by the whole thing.

### Concepts taught

- the NMJL card is the source of legal winning hands
- SuitUp does not reproduce the card
- you choose a target hand from your own card
- winning requires an exact match to one card hand

### Why it comes here

The learner now has tiles in hand and needs a reason to care which tiles matter.

### Concrete action

Ask the learner to pick one practice target hand category from their own card and say:
"I am aiming for this, not just collecting random matches."

## Lesson 7: Run the Charleston

### Goal

The learner can execute the passing sequence in the correct order.

### Concepts taught

- first Charleston:
 right, across, left
- optional courtesy pass:
 across, up to 3, only if both players agree
- second Charleston:
 left, across, right
- no jokers are passed
- standard passes are exactly 3 tiles
- courtesy pass may be fewer than 3 tiles

### Why it comes here

The Charleston changes a hand before normal play starts.
A beginner must learn it before the draw-discard loop.

### Concrete action

Ask the learner to complete a guided mock Charleston using placeholder tiles.

The lesson ends when the learner can recite the order correctly and perform one full sequence without prompts.

## Lesson 8: Understand the turn loop

### Goal

The learner understands what happens on a normal turn.

### Concepts taught

- draw
- check whether the hand is complete
- discard if not declaring Mah Jongg
- play proceeds counterclockwise

### Why it comes here

The turn loop is the heartbeat of the hand.

### Concrete action

Ask the learner to perform one full practice turn:
draw one tile, evaluate, then discard one tile.

## Lesson 9: Learn groups and exposures

### Goal

The learner can identify basic group types and understand what it means to expose them.

### Concepts taught

- pair
- pung
- kong
- quint
- sextet
- exposed versus concealed tiles

### Why it comes here

A player must know what they are building before deciding whether to call a discard.

### Concrete action

Ask the learner to sort example tiles into:
- pair
- pung
- kong
- not a valid matching group yet

Then ask the learner to move one sample group into an "exposed" area.

## Lesson 10: Call for an exposure

### Goal

The learner understands that a discard can interrupt normal play.

### Concepts taught

- a discarded tile can sometimes be called
- the call creates an exposure
- exposures reveal information to the table
- calls should support the exact hand being pursued

### Why it comes here

This is the first moment where passive drawing becomes active tactical choice.

### Concrete action

Present one discard and ask:
"Would you call this for your target hand or let it go?"

The lesson ends when the learner gives a reason tied to the target hand, not just "because it matches."

## Lesson 11: Learn joker limits

### Goal

The learner stops treating jokers as universal wild cards.

### Concepts taught

- jokers may be used in pungs, kongs, quints, and sextets
- jokers may not be used in pairs
- jokers may not be used in singles

### Why it comes here

Beginners almost always overgeneralize jokers.
This lesson prevents a foundational error.

### Concrete action

Show several candidate groups and ask the learner to label each as:
- joker legal
- joker not legal

The lesson ends when the learner correctly rejects jokers in pairs and singles.

## Lesson 12: Practice joker redemption

### Goal

The learner understands how a real tile can replace a joker in an exposed group.

### Concepts taught

- redemption applies to an exposed group
- the real tile replaces the joker
- the redeemed joker returns to the redeemer's hand
- redemption changes options without changing the fact that the group stays exposed

### Why it comes here

This mechanic is easier once exposures and joker restrictions are already understood.

### Concrete action

Show an exposed group containing a joker and ask the learner to perform the exchange with the real tile.

## Lesson 13: Win only with an exact hand

### Goal

The learner internalizes the single most important American Mah Jongg truth.

### Concepts taught

- a tidy rack is not enough
- several matching groups are not enough
- being one tile away is not enough
- only one exact completed NMJL card hand counts as Mah Jongg

### Why it comes here

By now the learner has enough mechanics to understand why exactness matters.

### Concrete action

Show two near-complete racks:
- one exact legal match
- one almost-right rack

Ask the learner to choose which one may declare Mah Jongg and explain why.

## Lesson 14: Guided practice patterns

### Goal

The learner rehearses simplified hand-building before full free play.

### Concepts taught

- choosing a target
- keeping useful tiles
- passing unwanted tiles
- noticing when a discard helps the target
- protecting pairs and required singles because jokers cannot replace them

### Why it comes here

Practice patterns create a bridge between isolated lessons and a full hand.

### Concrete action

Give the learner a small guided scenario with a stated target and ask them to make:
- one Charleston pass choice
- one keep/discard choice
- one call/no-call choice

## Lesson 15: Full practice play

### Goal

The learner plays a complete beginner practice hand from setup through Mah Jongg or dead-end practice completion.

### Concepts taught

This lesson integrates everything:
- tile recognition
- wall building
- dealer selection
- dealing
- target selection from the learner's own NMJL card
- Charleston
- draw/discard loop
- exposures
- joker limits
- redemption
- exact-hand winning

### Why it is last

A full hand should feel like a synthesis, not an ambush.

### Concrete action

Ask the learner to complete one full practice session with guidance reduced to prompts rather than explanations.

The lesson ends when the learner can narrate their own decisions in simple terms:
- "This is my target."
- "I passed these because they do not fit."
- "I kept this pair because a joker cannot replace it."
- "I can declare Mah Jongg because this exactly matches my chosen hand."

## Difficulty progression

SuitUp should teach in three practice bands.

### Band 1: Fully guided

The app highlights:
- what tiles are being named
- where walls go
- whose turn it is
- legal versus illegal joker use
- Charleston direction
- likely next action

The learner is not rushed.

### Band 2: Prompted practice

The app reduces hints and asks the learner to decide:
- what to pass
- what to keep
- whether a group is exposed or concealed
- whether a joker is legal
- whether a rack is truly complete

### Band 3: Supported play

The learner plays nearly independently against AI opponents while the app remains available for:
- rule reminders
- mistake correction
- post-turn explanations
- end-of-hand reflection

## Error-handling philosophy

When the learner makes a mistake, SuitUp should correct gently and specifically.

Good beginner feedback:
- "That joker cannot be used there because pairs must be real tiles."
- "You passed a joker, but jokers are not passed in the Charleston."
- "This rack is close, but it does not exactly match a hand on your NMJL card yet."

Bad beginner feedback:
- "Illegal move."
- "Wrong."
- "Try again."

The goal is calm correction, not punishment.

## Pacing rules

To stay beginner-safe, first release pedagogy should follow these pacing rules:

- Introduce only one new rules concept per lesson whenever possible.
- Keep explanations short enough to act on immediately.
- Repeat important constraints often:
 exact hand, joker limits, Charleston order.
- Prefer doing over reading.
- Use visible progress so the learner feels momentum.
- Unlock full practice only after the learner has completed the prerequisite drills.

## Physical-set teaching notes

Because the learner owns a real set, SuitUp should repeatedly connect screen instruction to table action.

Examples:
- "Find all four East tiles."
- "Build one wall 19 tiles long."
- "Place this exposed pung in front of your rack."
- "Swap the real tile for the joker in that exposed group."

The app is a teacher, not a replacement for touching the tiles.

## What success looks like

A successful beginner does not need perfect strategy.

A successful beginner can:
- identify the tile categories
- set up the table
- choose a dealer by wind draw
- deal the opening counts correctly
- perform the Charleston in order
- explain the draw-evaluate-discard loop
- recognize exposures
- apply joker limits correctly
- understand joker redemption
- use a personal NMJL card as the winning reference
- declare Mah Jongg only on an exact legal hand

## Release-scope guardrails

First release pedagogy should not try to do everything.

It should not:
- reproduce copyrighted NMJL card hands
- drown the learner in advanced table politics
- assume prior card-game fluency
- front-load strategy before mechanics
- treat memorization as the first skill
- shame the learner for slow play or repeated drills

## Curriculum summary

SuitUp teaches American Mah Jongg in the order a calm beginner can absorb it:

1. identify tiles
2. name tiles
3. build walls
4. choose dealer
5. deal opening hands
6. choose a target from the learner's own NMJL card
7. run the Charleston
8. perform the turn loop
9. understand groups and exposures
10. call exposures intentionally
11. apply joker limits
12. practice joker redemption
13. verify exact-hand wins
14. rehearse guided practice patterns
15. play a full guided practice hand

That sequence keeps the learner moving from recognition to action, from action to understanding, and from understanding to confident play.
