# SuitUp Pedagogy — Release 1 Curriculum Structure

Version: Release 1
Last updated: July 4, 2026
Audience: Engineers, content authors, curriculum designers, and future maintainers of the SuitUp teaching flow

## Purpose

This document defines how SuitUp teaches a total beginner to play American Mah Jongg in Release 1.

SuitUp is not a reference manual first. It is a patient, structured teacher. The curriculum begins with physical setup and concrete table actions before asking the learner to reason about turn flow, passing, exposure, or winning patterns. That ordering is deliberate.

The product goal is simple:

A beginner who starts with a physical set and no prior knowledge should be able to move from "I do not know what these tiles are" to "I can set up a table, participate in the Charleston, take legal turns, and finish a guided practice hand."

This document maps the first-release curriculum into Units, Lessons, and numbered Steps. It also explains the teaching principle that every lesson must end with something the learner can actually do at a real table.

## Teaching stance

SuitUp teaches like a patient instrument tutor or skills coach:

- begin with visible, touchable actions
- introduce one new concept at a time
- reinforce vocabulary only when it is attached to an action
- keep lessons short enough that success feels close
- end every lesson with a concrete capability, not just recognition
- alternate explanation with immediate practice

Release 1 assumes the learner may have:

- never played any form of Mah Jongg
- never seen an American Mah Jongg rack, wall, or Charleston
- a real physical set in front of them but no confidence using it
- anxiety about "slowing down the table" or doing something wrong
- no NMJL card knowledge yet beyond knowing that such a card exists

That beginner assumption matters. We do not start with hand theory, scoring nuances, or card interpretation drills. We start with the physical table because the beginner's first barrier is not optimization. It is orientation.

## Core pedagogical rule: always end on something you can do

Every lesson in SuitUp must end with a concrete action the learner can perform without help.

That means a lesson is not complete when the learner can merely define a term. A lesson is complete when the learner can take an action at the table or inside the practice interface.

Examples of acceptable lesson endings:

- sort tiles by suit
- identify all Winds and Dragons
- build their section of the wall
- choose the dealer using the taught method
- pass the correct number of tiles in a Charleston phase
- draw a tile and discard a tile in order
- recognize whether a group is a pair, pung, kong, or quint
- decide whether a joker is allowed in a given group
- call for a discard legally in a guided scenario
- declare Mah Jongg in a guided practice hand

Examples of unacceptable lesson endings:

- "understand the idea of the wall"
- "know what the Charleston is"
- "be familiar with exposure"
- "be introduced to jokers"

If the learner cannot do something observable after the lesson, the lesson is too abstract and must be rewritten.

## Why the sequence starts with setup and dealer selection

Release 1 begins with physical setup, tile identity, seating, wall building, and dealer selection before the Charleston or live turns for four reasons.

### 1. Beginners need spatial orientation before procedural flow

American Mah Jongg is table-based and position-based. Terms like dealer, pass left, pass across, exposure, wall, and discards only make sense after the learner can see where tiles go and where players sit.

A beginner who does not yet know:

- where their rack belongs
- how the wall is built
- who is East
- what a discard area looks like

cannot confidently execute the Charleston or a live draw-discard turn.

### 2. Physical confidence lowers cognitive load

The first fear most beginners have is "I do not even know how to set this up."

If SuitUp solves that fear first, the learner arrives at turn-flow lessons with less stress. They are no longer juggling tile names, seat roles, and physical arrangement at the same time.

### 3. Dealer selection creates the first table role anchor

The learner needs a stable mental model of table orientation:

- one player is dealer
- dealer position matters
- passes and turn order depend on orientation

Dealer selection is therefore not trivia. It is the anchor for later concepts such as first deal, turn order, and table-relative passing.

### 4. Charleston only makes sense after the learner can hold and sort a starting hand

The Charleston is not just "passing tiles." It is selective passing from an actual rack. To make good sense of it, the learner must first know:

- what tiles look like
- how a rack is organized
- how to notice duplicates, near-groups, and isolated tiles
- how table directions relate to pass instructions

That is why Charleston comes after setup, orientation, and basic grouping recognition.

## Product teaching modes

Release 1 uses two complementary modes:

1. Walkthrough Mode
2. Practice-Table Mode

These modes teach the same curriculum from different angles.

### Walkthrough Mode

Walkthrough Mode is the guided teacher.

It introduces new concepts in a fixed order, uses constrained scenarios, labels table regions clearly, and prevents avoidable confusion. In this mode, SuitUp can:

- highlight tiles
- restrict actions to the current legal or instructional choice set
- narrate why a step happens
- pause after each small action
- repeat terminology while the learner acts

Walkthrough Mode is where first exposure happens.

### Practice-Table Mode

Practice-Table Mode is the rehearsal space.

It gives the learner room to repeat what was taught with lighter scaffolding. In this mode, the learner applies the lesson outcome in a playable table environment. Practice-Table Mode can:

- replay a learned setup sequence
- run a focused Charleston drill
- run draw-discard repetitions
- present legal-call scenarios
- allow AI opponents to complete the table rhythm

Practice-Table Mode is where confidence is built.

### How the modes reinforce each other

The reinforcement loop is intentional:

- Walkthrough Mode introduces a small concept.
- The learner performs that concept immediately.
- Practice-Table Mode repeats the same concept with less narration.
- The next Walkthrough lesson builds on what was just rehearsed.

This creates a cycle of:

explain -> demonstrate -> perform -> rehearse -> combine

That loop is the backbone of Release 1.

A lesson is not considered production-ready unless it has both:

- a Walkthrough version that teaches the concept clearly
- a Practice-Table reinforcement activity that lets the learner repeat it

## Beginner assumptions

Release 1 content must assume all of the following unless a lesson explicitly says otherwise:

- the learner does not know the tile families yet
- the learner does not know American Mah Jongg table vocabulary
- the learner may confuse seat direction with pass direction
- the learner may not know when a tile group is complete
- the learner may overestimate what jokers can do
- the learner may think winning means "any four groups and a pair" without pattern restrictions
- the learner may be using Flowers/Seasons physically present in the box even though they are optional in SuitUp and off by default
- the learner may own a real NMJL card but not know how to use it yet

Because of those assumptions, every early lesson must:

- define the table state visually
- use one source of novelty at a time
- avoid stacking multiple exceptions in the same lesson
- restate the end goal in plain language
- end with one observable table action

## Curriculum map for Release 1

The first-release curriculum is organized as:

- Unit
- Lesson
- numbered Steps
- lesson completion action
- linked practice-table reinforcement

The units below define the canonical order for Release 1.

---

## Unit 1 — Meet the set and the table

### Goal

Help the learner become physically comfortable with the components before any live game procedure begins.

### Lesson 1.1 — What comes in the box

#### Steps

1. Show the complete tile families used in SuitUp: suits, Winds, Dragons, Jokers, and optional Flowers/Seasons.
2. Explain the exact role of each family in beginner language.
3. Identify that SuitUp teaches with Flowers/Seasons optional and off by default.
4. Show how racks, pushers if present, and table space are used during play.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can separate tiles into suits, Winds, Dragons, Jokers, and optional Flowers/Seasons using a real or simulated set.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A timed or untimed sorting drill where the learner places tiles into the correct family buckets.

### Lesson 1.2 — Reading the suits and honors

#### Steps

1. Introduce Dots, Bams, and Craks as the three numbered suits.
2. Show numbers 1 through 9 within each suit.
3. Introduce Winds: East, South, West, North.
4. Introduce Dragons: Red, Green, White.
5. Contrast numbered tiles with honor tiles.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can name a shown tile correctly and distinguish a suit tile from a Wind or Dragon.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Tile identification prompts with immediate feedback.

### Lesson 1.3 — Rack, table space, and discard area

#### Steps

1. Show where each player's rack sits.
2. Show where walls are built.
3. Show where discards go.
4. Show where exposures are placed.
5. Explain that table layout supports later rules.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can place rack tiles, wall tiles, and discards in the correct table zones.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A drag-and-place layout exercise.

---

## Unit 2 — Set up a real table

### Goal

Teach the learner to physically prepare a playable table.

### Lesson 2.1 — Seat the players and orient the table

#### Steps

1. Show a four-player table.
2. Explain that table position matters.
3. Introduce East as the dealer position anchor.
4. Label left, across, and right relative to the learner's seat.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can identify their left, across, and right neighbors at the table and locate East.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Seat-direction drills using rotating table prompts.

### Lesson 2.2 — Choose the dealer

#### Steps

1. Explain why the table needs a dealer before the hand begins.
2. Teach the Release 1 beginner dealer-selection procedure used by SuitUp.
3. Show the selected dealer becoming East.
4. Reinforce that dealer status anchors turn order and later passes.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can complete the taught dealer-selection procedure and identify who is East.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A repeatable setup scenario where the learner assigns East correctly.

### Lesson 2.3 — Shuffle and build the wall

#### Steps

1. Show all tiles face down for mixing.
2. Demonstrate the shuffle phase.
3. Demonstrate building each player's section of the wall.
4. Show how the four wall sections form the table boundary.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can help shuffle tiles and build their wall section correctly.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A guided wall-building drill with visual correction.

---

## Unit 3 — Form a hand and organize the rack

### Goal

Move from setup into the learner's first usable rack.

### Lesson 3.1 — Deal the starting tiles

#### Steps

1. Introduce the beginner dealing sequence used in SuitUp.
2. Show tiles moving from the wall to players.
3. Explain what a starting rack looks like.
4. Pause once the learner has a full starting hand.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can recognize when the initial deal is complete and arrange the received tiles on their rack.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A mock deal where the learner verifies final rack count and readiness.

### Lesson 3.2 — Sort a rack for thinking

#### Steps

1. Show common beginner-friendly sorting methods.
2. Group suit tiles by suit and number.
3. Group honors together.
4. Place Jokers where they remain visible.
5. Point out duplicate tiles and near-groups.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can sort a rack into a readable layout that helps make decisions.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Rack-organization drills using messy starting hands.

### Lesson 3.3 — Spot pairs and groups

#### Steps

1. Define pair, pung, kong, and quint.
2. Show examples with tiles on the rack.
3. Explain that some groups are complete and some are only promising.
4. Introduce the idea that group recognition drives later decisions.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can point to a pair, pung, kong, or quint in a sample rack.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A group-spotting exercise with increasing complexity.

---

## Unit 4 — Learn the Charleston

### Goal

Teach the learner the passing ritual in manageable pieces.

### Lesson 4.1 — What the Charleston is for

#### Steps

1. Explain that the Charleston is an organized passing sequence before live play.
2. Clarify that its purpose is to improve the starting rack.
3. Connect pass directions to table orientation learned earlier.
4. Show that the Charleston happens before ordinary draw-discard turns.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can explain when the Charleston happens and why players do it.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A simple identify-the-phase drill that distinguishes setup, Charleston, and live turns.

### Lesson 4.2 — Pass left, across, right

#### Steps

1. Teach the first directional pass.
2. Teach the second directional pass.
3. Teach the third directional pass.
4. Visually highlight the destination of each pass.
5. Repeat until the learner stops confusing table-relative directions.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can pass the correct number of tiles in the correct direction for each taught phase.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A Charleston direction drill with immediate correctness feedback.

### Lesson 4.3 — Choose what to pass

#### Steps

1. Show how to identify weak, isolated, or mismatched tiles.
2. Contrast useful keeps with likely passes.
3. Demonstrate beginner-safe passing logic without advanced strategy overload.
4. Reinforce that the goal is improvement, not perfection.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can choose three tiles to pass and give a simple reason for each choice.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Rack evaluation mini-scenarios focused only on pass decisions.

### Lesson 4.4 — Finish the Charleston and reset for live play

#### Steps

1. Run a complete beginner Charleston sequence in order.
2. Show the learner receiving passed tiles.
3. Prompt the learner to re-sort the rack.
4. Mark the table as ready for live turns.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can complete a guided Charleston from start to finish and prepare the rack for play.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A full Charleston rehearsal with reduced hints.

---

## Unit 5 — Play legal turns

### Goal

Teach the rhythm of the hand once passing is over.

### Lesson 5.1 — Draw, evaluate, discard

#### Steps

1. Explain the basic turn rhythm.
2. Show where the drawn tile comes from.
3. Add the tile to the rack.
4. Evaluate whether it helps.
5. Discard one tile to end the turn.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can take a legal draw-discard turn in the correct order.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A repeated turn loop against passive opponents.

### Lesson 5.2 — What exposures are

#### Steps

1. Define exposure as placing a called or declared group face up.
2. Show where exposed groups sit.
3. Contrast exposed tiles with concealed tiles on the rack.
4. Explain that exposures change what the table can see.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can identify whether a shown group is concealed or exposed.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A classification drill with table examples.

### Lesson 5.3 — Calls and interruptions

#### Steps

1. Explain that some discards can be called.
2. Show a guided legal-call situation.
3. Explain how the call interrupts ordinary turn flow.
4. Place the resulting exposure correctly on the table.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can recognize a guided legal call and place the resulting exposure correctly.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Scenario cards where the learner chooses whether to call or pass.

---

## Unit 6 — Understand jokers and group limits

### Goal

Teach the most common beginner source of confusion before it causes bad habits.

### Lesson 6.1 — Where jokers are allowed

#### Steps

1. Introduce Jokers as wild tiles with limits.
2. Show the kinds of groups where Jokers are allowed in SuitUp's beginner rules teaching.
3. Show examples of legal Joker use in groups.
4. Contrast with examples where Joker use is not allowed.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can tell whether a Joker is allowed in a shown group example.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A legal-or-illegal Joker placement drill.

### Lesson 6.2 — Joker caution for beginners

#### Steps

1. Explain why beginners often overuse Jokers mentally.
2. Show that Jokers do not erase all structure requirements.
3. Re-anchor group recognition using mixed natural and Joker examples.
4. Reinforce table checking before exposure.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can correct an illegal beginner Joker assumption in a sample scenario.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Short error-correction prompts.

---

## Unit 7 — Know what counts as progress toward winning

### Goal

Introduce winning structure carefully without reproducing copyrighted card material.

### Lesson 7.1 — Practice patterns versus the real card

#### Steps

1. Explain that standard American Mah Jongg uses a real yearly NMJL card.
2. State clearly that SuitUp does not reproduce that copyrighted card.
3. Introduce SuitUp practice patterns as original teaching material.
4. Explain that these practice patterns train recognition of groups, timing, and legal play.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can explain the difference between a real NMJL card and a SuitUp practice pattern.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A compare-and-identify prompt using plain-language examples.

### Lesson 7.2 — Read a beginner practice objective

#### Steps

1. Show a simple original practice pattern objective.
2. Identify required groups in plain language.
3. Show how the learner checks a rack against that objective.
4. Emphasize that not every attractive rack is a winning rack.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can compare a rack to a beginner practice objective and describe what is still missing.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Goal-matching drills with rack snapshots.

### Lesson 7.3 — When you can say Mah Jongg

#### Steps

1. Explain that a win requires meeting a complete allowed pattern, not just general improvement.
2. Show a guided completed example.
3. Show a near-miss example that is not yet complete.
4. Prompt the learner to decide whether the hand is ready.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can distinguish a complete guided winning hand from a near-miss.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Win-or-not-yet scenarios.

---

## Unit 8 — Play a full guided hand

### Goal

Combine setup, passing, turns, calls, Joker rules, and winning recognition into one coherent experience.

### Lesson 8.1 — Guided table from setup to first discard

#### Steps

1. Seat the table.
2. Choose the dealer.
3. Build walls.
4. Deal the tiles.
5. Sort the rack.
6. Complete the Charleston.
7. Begin live turns.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can move from a fresh table to the first live discard without external explanation.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A reduced-hint start-of-hand simulation.

### Lesson 8.2 — Mid-hand decision support

#### Steps

1. Present several turns from a developing hand.
2. Ask the learner to choose keeps and discards.
3. Introduce one or two call decisions.
4. Reinforce legal sequencing under light pressure.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can complete several consecutive legal turns while keeping a beginner objective in mind.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A checkpoint simulation with optional hint toggles.

### Lesson 8.3 — Finish the hand

#### Steps

1. Bring the learner to a guided endgame state.
2. Prompt the learner to recognize completion.
3. Have the learner declare the win in the correct moment.
4. Review the path from setup to finish.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can complete a guided hand and identify why it qualifies as a win in SuitUp practice.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

A full-hand replay with fewer prompts.

---

## Unit 9 — Graduate into AI table practice

### Goal

Transition from fully guided learning to repeatable, confidence-building play.

### Lesson 9.1 — Practice against beginner AI

#### Steps

1. Explain the beginner AI's teaching purpose.
2. Start a low-pressure practice hand.
3. Encourage legal sequencing over strategy perfection.
4. Surface hints after mistakes instead of front-loading them.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can complete a playable hand against beginner AI with support enabled.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Free practice with full hinting.

### Lesson 9.2 — Intermediate AI and lighter scaffolding

#### Steps

1. Reduce narration.
2. Expect the learner to manage setup and Charleston with fewer prompts.
3. Present stronger discard pressure from opponents.
4. Encourage self-correction before hint use.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can manage most table flow independently in a lightly guided game.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Standard practice table with optional on-demand hints only.

### Lesson 9.3 — Advanced AI and replay review

#### Steps

1. Run a stronger AI table with legal and more purposeful play.
2. Capture key learner decisions.
3. Review discard, pass, and call moments after the hand.
4. Turn mistakes into named skills to revisit.

#### Lesson must end with

The learner can finish a hand, review major decisions, and choose the next skill to practice.

#### Practice-table reinforcement

Advanced practice with post-hand review.

## Canonical lesson structure template

Every Release 1 lesson should use the same internal structure.

### 1. Lesson promise

Start by telling the learner what they will be able to do by the end.

Example style:

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to pass tiles left, across, and right during a beginner Charleston.

### 2. Why this matters now

Explain why the skill belongs at this exact point in the sequence.

### 3. Numbered Steps

Present the procedure in small numbered actions. Each step should introduce one instructional burden at a time.

### 4. Guided check

Ask the learner to perform or identify something immediately.

### 5. Concrete completion action

End with the single action that proves lesson completion.

### 6. Practice-table reinforcement

Offer a repeatable drill that uses the same skill with fewer prompts.

## Step-writing rules

Within each lesson, numbered Steps must obey the following rules:

- Step 1 must orient the learner to the visible state.
- No step should require vocabulary that has not yet been introduced unless the interface labels it directly.
- A step should ask for one action or one observation, not both unless tightly linked.
- If a step contains an exception, the next step must show that exception in context.
- If a step changes table state, the UI should reflect that change immediately.
- The final step must hand off cleanly to the completion action.

## Difficulty pacing rules

Release 1 uses a slow-to-fast pacing model.

### Early lessons

Early lessons should:

- minimize branching
- minimize exceptions
- show one right action clearly
- let the learner repeat obvious physical motions

### Middle lessons

Middle lessons should:

- combine two learned skills
- begin asking the learner to choose among legal options
- introduce common mistakes and corrections

### Later lessons

Later lessons should:

- preserve legal clarity while reducing prompts
- expect learner memory across multiple phases of a hand
- use AI pressure to reinforce rhythm without punishing inexperience

## What not to do in Release 1

To preserve beginner trust, lessons in Release 1 must not:

- start with hand taxonomy before the learner can sort a rack
- teach the Charleston before left/across/right orientation is stable
- bury legal restrictions in long paragraphs
- force card-study before basic table flow is learned
- assume prior knowledge of NMJL abbreviations or annual card conventions
- treat mistakes as failure states rather than teaching moments
- end a lesson with abstract comprehension only

## Release 1 acceptance checklist for lesson authors

A lesson is ready for inclusion only if the answer to every question below is yes.

- Does the lesson promise one clear capability?
- Is the capability appropriate for a total beginner?
- Does the lesson come after all required prerequisites?
- Are the Steps numbered and action-oriented?
- Does the lesson end with one observable thing the learner can do?
- Is there a matching practice-table reinforcement activity?
- Does the lesson avoid copyrighted NMJL card reproduction?
- Does the lesson use plain language before shorthand?
- Can the learner succeed even if they started with no table vocabulary?

## Summary of the teaching philosophy

SuitUp teaches American Mah Jongg the way a good beginner coach teaches any tactile skill:

- start with the equipment
- anchor the learner in space
- introduce one action at a time
- rehearse before increasing pressure
- end every lesson with a real capability

If the learner finishes a lesson but still cannot do anything at the table, the lesson has failed.

If the learner finishes a lesson and can perform one clear action with confidence, the curriculum is working.
