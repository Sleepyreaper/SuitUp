## The tile set

American Mah Jongg uses 152 tiles.

### Suits: 108 tiles total

There are three numbered suits:
- Dots, also called Circles
- Bams, also called Bamboo
- Craks, also called Characters

Each suit has numbers 1 through 9.
There are four of each numbered tile.

That gives:
- 9 Dot ranks x 4 = 36 tiles
- 9 Bam ranks x 4 = 36 tiles
- 9 Crak ranks x 4 = 36 tiles

Total suit tiles: 108

### Winds: 16 tiles total

There are four winds:
- East
- South
- West
- North

There are four of each wind.

Total wind tiles: 16

### Dragons: 12 tiles total

There are three dragons:
- Red
- Green
- White

There are four of each dragon.

Total dragon tiles: 12

### Flowers: 8 tiles total

There are eight flowers.

In American Mah Jongg, flowers are usually interchangeable for hand requirements that call for flowers.

Total flower tiles: 8

### Jokers: 8 tiles total

There are eight jokers.

Jokers are special wild tiles, but only in certain kinds of groups. They are powerful and restricted.

Total joker tiles: 8

### Total

- Suits: 108
- Winds: 16
- Dragons: 12
- Flowers: 8
- Jokers: 8

Grand total: 152 tiles

## Core idea of the game

In American Mah Jongg, you do not win by making just any legal-looking collection of tiles.

You win only if your concealed and exposed tiles match one exact hand pattern from your current NMJL card.

That is the central beginner idea:

1. Read the current card.
2. Pick a hand category or specific line to pursue.
3. Build exactly that hand through draws, discards, passing, and calls.
4. Declare Mah Jongg only when your tiles satisfy one exact hand on the card.

If your tiles do not match a complete hand on the card, you have not won.

## Basic group definitions

These terms matter because the Charleston, exposures, joker use, and winning all depend on them.

### Pair

A pair is two identical tiles.

Example:
- two 7 Dots

### Pung

A pung is three identical tiles.

Example:
- three Norths

### Kong

A kong is four identical tiles.

Example:
- four Red Dragons

### Quint

A quint is five identical tiles.

Example:
- five 3 Bams, usually involving joker use if allowed by the hand and by joker rules

### Sextet

A sextet is six identical tiles.

Example:
- six White Dragons, if the hand pattern specifically allows it and joker rules permit it

### Singles

A single is one tile by itself.

Singles matter while building toward a hand, especially before you have formed complete groups.

## Walls and table setup

At the start of a hand, all tiles are mixed face down.

Then players build walls.

With four players, the standard setup is:

- each player builds a wall 19 tiles long and 2 tiles high
- each wall therefore contains 38 tiles
- four walls together contain all 152 tiles

Players push the four walls together to form a square.

This wall square becomes the source of dealt tiles and later draws.

## Choosing the dealer

Dealer is chosen by drawing winds.

A simple beginner procedure is:

1. Put one each of East, South, West, and North face down.
2. Each player draws one wind.
3. The player who draws East is the dealer.

After dealer is determined, players sit in wind order around the table.

For beginner practice, SuitUp teaches dealer selection by wind draw because it is clear and physical.

## Dealing tiles

Once the walls are built and the dealer is known, tiles are dealt.

SuitUp uses these beginner counts:

- the dealer starts with 14 tiles
- each other player starts with 13 tiles

That means:
- dealer will discard first
- other players begin one tile shorter than the dealer

For a total beginner, the important fact is not the exact physical ritual of every stack taken from the wall. The important fact is the starting hand size:
- 14 for dealer
- 13 for everyone else

## Turn order

Play proceeds counterclockwise.

A normal turn follows this loop:

1. A player draws a tile.
2. The player checks whether the hand now matches an exact NMJL card hand.
3. If not declaring Mah Jongg, the player discards one tile face up.

Then the next player takes a turn, unless a discard is called for an exposure or for Mah Jongg under the applicable rules.

For beginner learning, remember this rhythm:
- draw
- evaluate
- discard

## The Charleston

The Charleston is a pre-play passing sequence used in American Mah Jongg.

Its purpose is to improve hands before normal drawing and discarding begins.

SuitUp teaches the Charleston in this exact order:

### First Charleston

1. Pass 3 tiles to the right.
2. Pass 3 tiles across.
3. Pass 3 tiles to the left.

### Optional courtesy pass

After the first Charleston, players may make a courtesy pass across.

This pass is:
- across the table
- up to 3 tiles, not necessarily exactly 3
- allowed only if both players agree

A player may pass 0, 1, 2, or 3 tiles in this courtesy pass if both players accept the exchange.

### Second Charleston

1. Pass 3 tiles to the left.
2. Pass 3 tiles across.
3. Pass 3 tiles to the right.

## Charleston restrictions

These beginner restrictions are essential:

- Passes are made simultaneously.
- Passed tiles should be tiles you do not want for your chosen hand direction.
- During the standard Charleston passes, you pass exactly 3 tiles.
- In the courtesy pass across, you may pass up to 3 tiles, including fewer than 3.
- A joker may not be passed during the Charleston.
- A tile that has already been passed to you in the same pass cannot be immediately re-passed in that same pass.

For a beginner, the Charleston is not random dumping. It is the first moment you commit to a likely hand direction based on your NMJL card and your starting tiles.

## Exposures

An exposure is a group of tiles that is placed face up on the rack because it has been called or declared.

Common beginner reasons a group becomes exposed:
- you call a discard to complete a pung, kong, quint, or sextet when allowed
- you later add tiles to complete a larger exposed group as allowed by play
- you reveal tiles as part of a winning declaration

SuitUp teaches the beginner principle first:
exposed tiles are public information, concealed tiles remain private until revealed or used in a call.

## Calling discarded tiles

A player may sometimes call a just-discarded tile to complete a needed group.

For beginner play, SuitUp teaches the key idea without drowning the learner in edge-case vocabulary:
- a call interrupts normal turn flow
- the called tile must complete a legal need in the hand
- the resulting exposed set is shown on the rack
- after making the call and exposure, the caller continues play and later discards as required unless the call completed Mah Jongg

The exact usefulness of a called tile depends on the current NMJL hand you are pursuing.

## Joker rules

Jokers are restricted wild tiles. They are not universal substitutes.

SuitUp teaches these exact beginner-safe rules:

### Where jokers may be used

Jokers may be used in:
- pungs
- kongs
- quints
- sextets

That means jokers help fill groups of three or more identical tiles.

### Where jokers may not be used

Jokers may not be used in:
- pairs
- singles

If a hand requires a pair, that pair must be made of the actual matching tiles.
If a hand requires a single tile, that single must be the real tile.

### Joker redemption

If a player has the actual tile represented by a joker in an exposed group, that player may redeem the joker by exchanging the real tile for the joker, following the game rules being practiced.

SuitUp teaches joker redemption as a visible, learnable mechanic:
- the exposed set stays exposed
- the real tile replaces the joker in that set
- the redeemed joker returns to the redeemer's hand for later legal use

For beginners, the main lesson is that jokers are powerful but controlled. They do not replace everything.

## Flowers

Flowers are special tiles often treated as interchangeable when a hand calls for flowers.

For beginner learning, SuitUp teaches flowers as their own tile category:
- there are 8 flowers
- they are not numbered suit tiles
- many hands treat any flower as satisfying a flower requirement

Always confirm the exact hand requirement on your personal NMJL card.

## Declaring Mah Jongg

You may declare Mah Jongg only when your tiles satisfy one exact hand on the current NMJL card.

That requirement is strict.

You do not win because:
- your rack looks organized
- you made several pungs
- you have many matching tiles
- you are one tile away from a pattern that is not complete

You win only when the hand is exactly complete according to one legal line on the card.

For a beginner, this is the habit to build:
- identify a target hand
- keep checking whether your tiles still fit it
- do not drift into a "pretty" rack that matches nothing exactly

## How to use this rules reference with a physical set

A simple practice flow is:

1. Sort your tiles by category:
 suits, winds, dragons, flowers, jokers
2. Practice naming each suit and honor tile out loud.
3. Build four walls of 19 stacks by 2 tiles high.
4. Choose dealer by wind draw.
5. Deal 14 to dealer and 13 to everyone else.
6. Read your NMJL card and pick a possible hand.
7. Run the Charleston in the exact order listed above.
8. Begin normal play:
 draw, evaluate, discard
9. Expose groups only when legally called.
10. Remember that jokers cannot stand in for pairs or singles.
11. Declare Mah Jongg only when you match one exact card hand.

## Beginner mistakes to avoid

### Mistake 1: Treating jokers as universal wilds

They are not.
They cannot be used in pairs or singles.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring the exact hand card

In American Mah Jongg, the current NMJL card is not optional reference material.
It is the target list of legal winning hands.

### Mistake 3: Forgetting Charleston direction

Memorize the sequence:
- right
- across
- left
- optional across courtesy pass
- left
- across
- right

### Mistake 4: Calling exposures too casually

Every exposure gives other players information about your hand direction.

### Mistake 5: Thinking "close enough" wins

It does not.
The hand must match one exact card hand.
