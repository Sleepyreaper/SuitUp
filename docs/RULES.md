## The tile set

American Mah Jongg uses a 152-tile set.

### Suits

There are three numbered suits. Each suit has tiles numbered 1 through 9, and there are four copies of each tile.

- Dots (also called Circles): 36 tiles
- Bams (also called Bamboo): 36 tiles
- Craks (also called Characters): 36 tiles

That makes 108 suit tiles total.

### Winds

There are four Winds, with four copies of each:

- East
- South
- West
- North

That makes 16 Wind tiles total.

### Dragons

There are three Dragon types, with four copies of each:

- Red
- Green
- White

That makes 12 Dragon tiles total.

### Jokers

There are 8 Joker tiles.

Jokers are special wild tiles, but only for certain kinds of groups. Their limits matter, and beginners should learn those limits early.

### Flowers and Seasons

There are 8 Flowers/Seasons.

In SuitUp, Flowers/Seasons are optional and off by default.

If you are learning with a physical set, you may see these tiles included in the box. SuitUp treats them as optional so that beginners can first learn the core rhythm of the game without extra setup variation.

### Total count

The full set described above is:

- 108 suit tiles
- 16 Winds
- 12 Dragons
- 8 Jokers
- 8 Flowers/Seasons

Total: 152 tiles

## Goal of the game

Your goal is to complete a legal winning hand by collecting the exact groups required by the hand you are playing toward.

In practical beginner terms:

- you draw and discard one tile at a time
- you try to improve your hand
- you may call certain discards when the rules allow
- you win when your tiles match a complete legal hand

In American Mah Jongg, a hand is not “close enough.” It must match a legal pattern exactly.

## Core vocabulary

### Pair

A pair is 2 identical tiles.

Example:
- two matching 5 Dots

### Pung

A pung is 3 identical tiles.

Example:
- three matching Norths

### Kong

A kong is 4 identical tiles.

Example:
- four matching Red Dragons

### Quint

A quint is 5 identical tiles.

A quint usually requires a Joker unless your set or table rules provide all needed matching tiles through play options being used. In beginner teaching, the key point is that a quint is a five-of-a-kind group.

### Single

A single is one tile by itself.

Some legal hands use singles, but many beginner practice hands focus more on pairs, pungs, kongs, and simple combinations.

### Exposure

An exposure is a group that has been placed face up on the table because it was called or declared.

If a group stays hidden on your rack, it is concealed. If it is placed out for everyone to see, it is exposed.

## Choosing the dealer

Before the hand starts, choose the first dealer.

A beginner-friendly way to do this is simple and fair:

- each player draws a tile
- compare according to your table method
- highest result becomes dealer

Once the dealer is chosen, seat order proceeds around the table.

If you are practicing solo in SuitUp, the app assigns the dealer for teaching flow.

## Building the wall

After the tiles are mixed face down, players build the wall.

Basic beginner procedure:

1. Place all tiles face down on the table.
2. Mix them thoroughly.
3. Each player builds a wall in front of them.
4. Push the walls together to form the full square or rectangle used for the hand.

The important beginner idea is that the wall is the shared draw supply for the hand.

## Dealing the tiles

After the wall is built, tiles are dealt to each player according to the table’s dealing procedure.

For beginners, the important concepts are:

- each player starts with a hand on their rack
- the dealer begins the hand
- play then continues one turn at a time
- players draw and discard as the hand progresses

When learning with real people, follow the dealing method your table uses for American Mah Jongg. SuitUp teaches the flow after the deal and helps you understand what to do once tiles are on your rack.

## The Charleston

The Charleston is a passing sequence at the start of American Mah Jongg. It helps shape hands before normal draw-and-discard play begins.

This is one of the most distinctive rituals in the game, so learn it slowly and exactly.

### What the Charleston is for

During the Charleston, players pass unwanted tiles and receive replacement tiles from other players. This helps everyone move toward a workable hand.

### General Charleston rule

When you are required to pass, you pass 3 tiles.

You choose tiles you do not want for your current direction of play.

### Order of passes

The Charleston follows this order:

1. pass to the right
2. pass across
3. pass to the left

That right-across-left rhythm is the main beginner pattern to remember.

### Prohibitions and limits

Do not improvise the Charleston.

For beginner play in SuitUp, the important prohibitions are:

- pass exactly the required number of tiles when a pass is taking place
- follow the Charleston order exactly
- do not pass tiles in a made-up direction
- do not create your own extra pass sequence
- do not use the Charleston to break rules about tile visibility or legal play

If your teaching table uses optional variations, learn the standard order first before adding anything extra.

## Normal turn flow after the Charleston

Once the Charleston is complete, normal play begins.

A typical turn looks like this:

1. Draw a tile.
2. Check whether the tile helps complete your intended hand.
3. If you can declare a legal win, do so.
4. If not, discard one tile.
5. The next player or another eligible player may respond if the discard can be legally called.
6. If nobody calls it, play continues.

For a total beginner, the most important habit is this: after your draw, decide what hand you are trying to make, then discard the tile that helps that plan the least.

## Calling tiles and exposures

Some discards can be called to complete groups, depending on the game situation and the type of hand you are making.

When you call a discard and place the completed set on the table, that set becomes an exposure.

Beginners should remember two things:

- exposed groups are visible to everyone
- exposing tiles gives information away, but it can also speed up your hand

SuitUp teaches when a call is legal in the guided lessons and rules engine.

## Joker rules

Jokers are powerful, but they are not unlimited wild cards.

### What jokers can do

Jokers can stand in for missing tiles in certain groups.

For beginner learning, the safe rule is:

- jokers are used in groups, not as free replacements for anything at all

### What jokers do not do

Jokers do not let you ignore the structure of a legal hand.

You still need the right number of tiles arranged into the right kinds of groups.

### Best beginner mental model

Treat a Joker as a substitute tile that helps complete an allowed group.

Do not treat it as “I can win with whatever I have.”

### Practical beginner caution

Before using a Joker in your planning, ask:

- what group is this Joker helping me complete?
- is that group actually part of the hand I am building?
- would I still understand my hand if I replaced the Joker with the real tile it stands for?

If the answer is unclear, your hand plan is probably unclear too.

## How to think about groups as a beginner

When you look at your rack, do not stare at all the tiles equally. Sort them mentally into possible groups.

Ask yourself:

- do I already have a pair?
- do I have two or three matching tiles that could grow into a pung or kong?
- do I have tiles that fit one suit cleanly?
- do my Winds, Dragons, and Jokers support the same plan?

Beginners improve much faster when they stop thinking “What is every tile doing?” and start thinking “What groups am I building?”

## Win condition

You win when your tiles form a complete legal hand.

That means:

- the hand structure is complete
- the groups are legal
- the tile counts are correct
- the final hand matches a valid hand pattern you are playing

In plain language: you do not win because your hand looks good. You win because it is exactly legal.

## Using your real NMJL card

This section teaches how to use your real NMJL card without reproducing copyrighted card content.

The real NMJL card is copyrighted and not included in SuitUp.

### What the card does

Your NMJL card tells you which exact hand patterns are legal for that year.

Each line on the card describes a hand goal made from specific tile arrangements. Those hands are organized into sections on the card.

### How a beginner should use it

Use the card as a menu of target hands.

1. Look across the card and choose one hand that seems simple enough to chase.
2. Identify the groups it requires.
3. Identify which suits, honors, pairs, or other specific tiles it needs.
4. Keep tiles that support that one plan.
5. Pass or discard tiles that clearly do not fit that plan.
6. Re-check your target hand often as your rack changes.

### What not to do

Do not try to memorize the whole card at once.

Do not switch target hands every turn unless your tiles force a change.

Do not reproduce card text into your own notes or software if you want to stay inside the copyright boundary.

### Best beginner approach

Pick one realistic hand early and commit to it long enough to learn why tiles matter.

As you improve, you will learn when to pivot to a different legal hand.

## Flowers and Seasons in beginner play

Some physical sets include Flowers and Seasons as part of the 152-tile set.

In SuitUp, Flowers/Seasons are optional and off by default.

That default is deliberate. New players usually learn faster when they first focus on:

- tile identification
- group building
- passing during the Charleston
- legal draw and discard rhythm
- exposures
- Joker discipline
- exact winning structure

Once those basics feel comfortable, you can enable Flowers/Seasons if your practice mode or home table uses them.

## Beginner checklist for a first real hand

Before the hand starts:

- confirm you have the full tile set
- remember that Flowers/Seasons are optional and off by default in SuitUp
- choose the dealer
- mix the tiles
- build the wall
- deal the tiles
- prepare for the Charleston

During the hand:

- choose a target hand from your real NMJL card
- pass 3 tiles in the correct Charleston order
- build toward clear groups
- use Jokers only within allowed group logic
- expose only when the rules allow
- draw, evaluate, and discard with purpose

To win:

- complete a fully legal hand
- make sure the final structure matches the target you chose

## Final beginner advice

American Mah Jongg feels complicated at first because several rituals happen before ordinary turns even begin.

That is normal.

Learn it in this order:

1. know the tiles
2. understand pairs and groups
3. practice setup and wall building
4. learn the Charleston order
5. learn draw and discard rhythm
6. learn how Jokers fit into groups
7. use your real NMJL card as your hand target guide

If you can do those seven things, you are no longer staring at a rack of mysterious tiles. You are actually playing.
