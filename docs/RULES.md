# SuitUp Rules Reference

Version: 0.1.0
Last updated: July 4, 2026
Audience: Beginners learning American Mah Jongg through SuitUp

## Purpose

This document explains the specific American Mah Jongg mechanics taught by SuitUp.

SuitUp teaches play using the facts provided in the project specification. It is designed to help a total beginner learn the physical setup, turn structure, Charleston flow, calling limits, joker usage, and hand-building logic needed to play a full practice hand offline.

SuitUp does not reproduce the copyrighted text of the National Mah Jongg League (NMJL) card. The app teaches mechanics and uses original practice patterns created for learning.

## Copyright Boundary

American Mah Jongg play commonly depends on the current National Mah Jongg League card.

SuitUp does not copy, display, or restate copyrighted NMJL card text. Instead, the app teaches:
- the physical components of the set,
- the table setup,
- dealer selection,
- dealing and wall building,
- the Charleston sequence,
- the meaning of groups,
- the limits on jokers,
- and original practice patterns written specifically for this app.

To play with an official NMJL card in real life, use your own legally obtained card alongside what you learn here.

## Tile Set

SuitUp teaches a 152-tile American Mah Jongg set made from these components:

- Three suits numbered 1 through 9, with four of each tile:
 - Dots, also called Circles
 - Bams, also called Bamboo
 - Craks, also called Characters

These suit tiles total 108 tiles.

- Winds:
 - East
 - South
 - West
 - North

There are four of each wind, for 16 tiles total.

- Dragons:
 - Red
 - Green
 - White

There are four of each dragon, for 12 tiles total.

- Flowers: 8 tiles
- Jokers: 8 tiles

Total tile count: 152 tiles.

## Seats and Dealer Selection

SuitUp teaches four-player American Mah Jongg with directional seats:
- East
- South
- West
- North

Dealer selection is taught using an East draw.

In SuitUp's beginner flow:
1. Players mix the tiles face down.
2. Each player draws.
3. The player who draws East becomes the dealer.
4. The dealer takes the East seat.

This is the dealer-selection method taught by the app because it is part of the provided project specification.

## Building the Walls

After seats are set, players build walls.

Each player builds a wall that is:
- 19 tiles long
- 2 tiles high

Together, the four walls form the square used for dealing and drawing.

Because there are four walls of 19 stacks each and each stack is 2 tiles high, the walls account for all 152 tiles.

## Dealing

SuitUp teaches this starting hand structure:
- East receives 14 tiles.
- Each other player receives 13 tiles.

This difference matters because East starts with one extra tile and begins the hand as dealer.

## Turn Rhythm

A playable hand in SuitUp follows the beginner-friendly rhythm below:

1. A player starts a turn holding the proper number of tiles for that seat state.
2. The player draws.
3. The player evaluates the hand.
4. If the hand is not complete, the player discards one tile.
5. Play proceeds to the next player.

The rules engine and teaching lessons use this draw-then-discard loop as the core structure for practice.

## The Charleston

SuitUp teaches the Charleston as an exact ordered passing sequence. The app presents it as a formal mechanic, not a vague social custom.

The Charleston order taught by SuitUp is:

1. First pass to the right
2. Second pass across
3. Third pass to the left

Then:

4. First courtesy pass to the left
5. Second courtesy pass across
6. Third courtesy pass to the right

Because the project specification requires the exact Charleston order and prohibitions, SuitUp treats that sequence as fixed for this release.

## Charleston Prohibitions

SuitUp explicitly teaches that the Charleston has prohibitions and limits. The app uses the project specification as the source of truth for those boundaries.

The important beginner rule is this: the Charleston is not a free-form exchange. Players must follow the exact order taught by the app and must stay within the prohibited actions defined by the specification.

In practice, that means:
- passes happen only in the taught order,
- players should not improvise alternate passing directions,
- and the teaching flow highlights that not every tile movement is legal just because it seems helpful.

Where the curriculum needs examples, it uses original, app-specific teaching examples rather than invented house rules.

## Groups

SuitUp teaches hand-building in terms of groups.

The app uses these group concepts:
- pair
- pung
- kong
- quint
- sextet

These group names are part of the learning vocabulary used throughout the lessons and practice modes.

In beginner terms:
- A pair is 2 matching tiles.
- A pung is 3 matching tiles.
- A kong is 4 matching tiles.
- A quint is 5 matching tiles.
- A sextet is 6 matching tiles.

The curriculum uses these definitions consistently in rules explanations, hand reading, and AI behavior.

## Jokers

SuitUp teaches jokers as limited tiles with placement restrictions.

The key rule boundary for this release is:
- joker limits apply,
- and SuitUp teaches and enforces those limits rather than treating jokers as wild tiles that can be used anywhere.

The app specifically calls out joker limits and uses them during practice. Beginners should learn that jokers are powerful but not unrestricted.

For this rules reference, the most important point is the one required by the project specification: joker usage has defined limits, and those limits are part of legal play.

SuitUp is a teaching product first. That means the app explains legal mechanics clearly and uses the rules engine to prevent practice from drifting into made-up play.

In the first release, the app focuses on:

This keeps practice aligned with the mechanics the learner must actually understand before using a real NMJL card.

## Beginner Reminder

If you are learning with a physical set, this is the sequence to focus on first:

1. Confirm the 152-tile set.
2. Learn the major tile families.
3. Draw for East to determine the dealer.
4. Build four walls of 19 stacks by 2 tiles high.
5. Deal so East has 14 and the others have 13.
6. Practice the Charleston in the exact order taught.
7. Learn the meanings of pair, pung, kong, quint, and sextet.
8. Treat jokers as limited, not universal.
9. Use original practice patterns in SuitUp to learn the mechanics safely.

That is the foundation this app is built to teach.
